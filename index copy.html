Cumulative Impact Survey for Sea Caves

A case study: The Canary Islands

CIMcaves

Background information

This survey aims to contribute to an investigation to enhance understanding of human impacts on the biological communities of sea caves, using the Canary Islands as a case study. To achieve this, the vulnerability values of this ecosystem to a range of anthropogenic stressors will be assessed. The information asked here consists of subjective ratings by each respondent on 7 vulnerability criteria for each stressor or human activity. While the survey is based on expert judgment, responses should consider the state of the art on each topic queried.

Prior experience or knowledge of the Canary Islands's marine ecosystems is not essential; in fact, greater diversity in participants' backgrounds is preferred. However, information will be requested regarding participants' familiarity with the biogeographical characteristics of the Canary region.

This survey is grounded in the theoretical framework of cumulative impact models first introduced by Benjamin Halpern and collaborators in 2007 and 2008, and subsequently expanded upon by other authors (B. Halpern et al., 2007, 2008, 2008b, 2009, 2015; Selkoe & Halpern, 2009, Andersen et al., 2013). See bibliography tag.

Instructions for filling up the survey

Once you have read this document and you feel ready click on the Let's start the survey! button at the top right of the webpage in green color. It will randomly open in another tab one of a set of prepared surveys. In this way, you can consult at any time the definitions of each criteria, its categories and examples that are in the Criteria & categories section. Each respondent can only take the survey once. You just have to select the value you consider for each of the 7 vulnerability criteria within each stressor or pressure-human activity combination.

If the survey random selection takes you to a version begining with the letter H as Survey version H1 it means that you are in a survey of the Halpern framework initially published in 2007 and 2008. This means that you will be asked about the vulnerability of communities living in sea caves. to a specific stressor, such as Agricultural fertilizers and pesticides discharged into the sea from ravine outlets (Ravine outlets) or Non-native species individuals released into the marine environment from offshore fishfarms cages (Alien species. Fishfarms) among others. An example, for the stressor Ravine discharge would be the question What is the vulnerability of sea caves to the stressor ravine outlets?, that is defined as Agricultural fertilizers and pesticides discharged into the sea from ravine outlets?. Note that stressors' definition is depicted in the survey.

If the survey random selection takes you to a version begining with the letter A as Survey version A1 you are looking at the Andersen framework for cumulative impacts published in Andersen et al. 2013. This means that the vulnerability values asked in this survey would be something like: What is the vulnerability of the biological communities that live in sea caves to a combination of a pressure and human activiticy?. Briefly, it can be explained as the vulnerability to a specific pressure generated by a human activity (See Pressures table). For example, given the combination of the activity Waster water discharge through urban seawage outfalls and the pressure organic matter, means What is the vulnerability to the organic matter generated by the waters discharged from the urban wastewater outfall?. The same activity can generate impact through other pressures, in this case through the introduction of nutrients or microbial pathogenic organisms. In summary, here we ask about the vulnerability of sea caves to different aspects of the impact generated by the same human activity.

Criteria and categories for evaluation

Please follow these criteria to establish values for each stressor/human activity:

  • Pressure distance: At what distance from the site of the activity will the pressure decrease to an insignificant level?
  • Frequency: How regular are occurrences of discrete threat events?
  • Functional impact: The trophic level where impact operates.
  • Resistance: The average tendency to resist changing its “natural” state in response to a threat.
  • Recovery time: Time required for restoration to the natural state once the threat has passed.
  • Certainty: Confidence in results based on personal experience and/or existing empirical studies.
  • Stressor's local knowledge: Degree of knowledge about the characteristics and quantification of a given stressor in the Canary Islands.
