Cumulative Impact Survey for Sea Caves

A case study: The Canary Islands

- CIMcaves investigation -

Background information

This survey aims to contribute to an investigation to enhance understanding of human impacts on the biological communities of sea caves, using The Canary Islands as a case study. To achieve this, the vulnerability values of this habitat to a range of anthropogenic stressors will be assessed. The information asked here consists of subjective ratings by each respondent on 7 vulnerability criteria for each stressor or human activity-pressure combination. While the survey is based on expert judgment, responses should consider the state of the art on each topic queried.

Prior experience or knowledge of The Canary Islands's marine ecosystems is not essential; in fact, greater diversity in participants' backgrounds is preferred. However, information will be requested regarding participants' familiarity with the biogeographical characteristics of the Canary region.

If there isn't a clear criterion regarding the vulnerability of marine cave habitats to one or several stressors, either because it's unknown or because there are no studies on the matter, values for a comparable habitat such as circalittoral or bathyal rocky bottoms, walls, etc., can be included. However, to indicate this, the Certainty of the answered should be set as "medium."

This survey is grounded in the theoretical framework of cumulative impact models first introduced by Benjamin Halpern and collaborators in 2007 and 2008, and subsequently expanded upon by other authors (B. Halpern et al., 2007, 2008, 2008b, 2009, 2015; Selkoe & Halpern, 2009, Andersen et al., 2013). See the Bibliography section below.

Instructions for filling up the survey

Once you have read this document and you feel ready click on the Let's start the survey! button at the top right of the webpage in green color. It will open another tab displaying a randomly selected survey from a prepared set. In this way, you can consult at any time in this document the definitions of each criteria, its categories and examples that are in the Criteria & categories section while filling up a survey in other tab. Each respondent can only take the survey once.

While completing the survey you just have to select the value you consider for each of the 7 vulnerability criteria within each stressor or pressure-human activity combination. Then just click on the Submit survey button in green at the end of the document.

If the survey random selection takes you to a version begining with the letter H as Version H1 it means that you are in a survey of the Halpern framework. This means that you will be asked about the vulnerability of communities living in sea caves to a specific stressor, p.e., for the stressor: Ravine discharge have to be interpreted as What is the vulnerability of sea caves' communities to agricultural nutrient input and pesticides discharged into the sea from ravine outlets.

If the survey random selection takes you to a version begining with the letter A as Version A1 you are looking at the Andersen framework. This means that the vulnerability values asked in this survey would be to a combination of a human activity and a pressure. p.e. for the combination Human activity: Ravine discharge and pressure Nutrient input have to be interpreted as What is the vulnerability of sea caves' communities to agricultural nutrient input discharged into the sea from ravine outlets?. Note that only one pressure is assessed in this question keeping "agricultural pestices" out of it. Also, keep in mind that same human activity can generate impacts through different pressures and the same pressure can be triggered by different human activities.

Criteria and categories for evaluation

Please follow these criteria to establish values for each stressor or human activity-pressure combination:

  • Pressure distance: At what distance from the site of the activity will the pressure decrease to an insignificant level?
  • Frequency: How regular are occurrences of discrete threat events?
  • Functional impact: The trophic level where impact operates.
  • Resistance: The average tendency to resist changing its “natural” state in response to a threat.
  • Recovery time: Time required for restoration to the natural state once the threat has passed.
  • Certainty: Confidence in results based on personal experience and/or existing empirical studies.
  • Stressor's local knowledge: Degree of knowledge about the characteristics and quantification of a given stressor in the Canary Islands.
